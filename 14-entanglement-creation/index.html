Entanglement Creation

Creating and Understanding Bell States

What is Entanglement?

Quantum entanglement is a phenomenon where two or more qubits become correlated in such a way that the quantum state of each qubit cannot be described independently. Measuring one qubit instantly determines the state of the other, regardless of the distance between them.

The "Spooky Action"

Einstein called entanglement "spooky action at a distance." While it doesn't allow faster-than-light communication, it enables quantum teleportation, superdense coding, and is essential for quantum computing advantage.

The Four Bell States

Bell states are the maximally entangled two-qubit states. They form an orthonormal basis for the two-qubit Hilbert space.

|Φ⁺⟩

(|00⟩ + |11⟩) / √2
Both qubits always measure the same.
Perfect correlation in Z-basis.

|Φ⁻⟩

(|00⟩ - |11⟩) / √2
Both qubits measure the same, with phase difference.
Anti-correlation in X-basis.

|Ψ⁺⟩

(|01⟩ + |10⟩) / √2
Qubits always measure opposite.
Perfect anti-correlation in Z-basis.

|Ψ⁻⟩

(|01⟩ - |10⟩) / √2
The singlet state. Anti-symmetric.
Used in quantum cryptography.

Why Entanglement Matters

Quantum Teleportation

Transfer quantum states using entanglement and classical communication.

Superdense Coding

Send 2 classical bits using 1 qubit and shared entanglement.

Quantum Cryptography

Detect eavesdroppers using entanglement correlations.

Quantum Computing

Entanglement enables exponential speedup for certain problems.

Key Takeaways

  • Bell states are maximally entangled two-qubit states
  • Created by H + CNOT circuit
  • Measuring one qubit instantly determines the other
  • Cannot be written as product of individual qubit states
  • Foundation for quantum communication and computation
