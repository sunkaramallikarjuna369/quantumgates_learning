Entanglement & Bell States

The Heart of Quantum Computing

Prerequisites - Understanding Entanglement

1. What is Entanglement?

Entanglement is a quantum correlation between particles where the state of one particle is fundamentally connected to the state of another, regardless of distance. Measuring one instantly determines the other.

2. Separable vs Entangled States

A state is SEPARABLE if it can be written as |ψ⟩⊗|φ⟩ (tensor product of individual states). If it CANNOT be factored this way, it's ENTANGLED. Example: |00⟩+|11⟩ cannot be written as (a|0⟩+b|1⟩)⊗(c|0⟩+d|1⟩).

3. The CNOT Gate Creates Entanglement

CNOT alone doesn't create entanglement from |00⟩. But CNOT applied after H on the control qubit creates entanglement: H⊗I on |00⟩ gives |+0⟩, then CNOT gives (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2 - a Bell state!

4. Bell States

The four Bell states are maximally entangled 2-qubit states. They form an orthonormal basis for 2-qubit systems. Any 2-qubit state can be expressed as a combination of Bell states.

Entanglement - The 4W+H Framework

WHAT is Entanglement?

Entanglement is a quantum phenomenon where two or more particles become correlated such that the quantum state of each particle cannot be described independently. The combined system has a definite state, but individual particles do not.

WHY is Entanglement Important?

Entanglement enables: quantum teleportation, superdense coding, quantum key distribution (QKD), quantum error correction, and exponential speedup in certain algorithms. It's the resource that makes quantum computers more powerful than classical ones.

WHEN does Entanglement Occur?

Entanglement is created when particles interact in specific ways. In quantum circuits: apply H to one qubit, then CNOT with that qubit as control. The key is creating correlations that cannot be explained classically.

WHERE is Entanglement Used?

Quantum algorithms (Shor's, Grover's), quantum communication (teleportation, QKD), quantum error correction (stabilizer codes), quantum sensing (improved precision), and fundamental physics experiments (Bell tests).

HOW to Create Entanglement?

Standard recipe: (1) Start with |00⟩, (2) Apply H to first qubit → |+0⟩, (3) Apply CNOT → |Φ⁺⟩ = (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2. Different initial states and gates create different Bell states.

Step-by-Step: Creating |Φ⁺⟩

Step 1: Start with |00⟩

|00⟩ = |0⟩ ⊗ |0⟩ = [1,0,0,0]ᵀ

Step 2: Apply H to first qubit

(H ⊗ I)|00⟩ = H|0⟩ ⊗ I|0⟩ = |+⟩ ⊗ |0⟩ = |+0⟩

|+0⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩)/√2 ⊗ |0⟩ = (|00⟩ + |10⟩)/√2

Step 3: Apply CNOT

CNOT flips the target when control is |1⟩:

CNOT|00⟩ = |00⟩ (control is 0, no flip)
CNOT|10⟩ = |11⟩ (control is 1, flip target)

Therefore:

CNOT(|00⟩ + |10⟩)/√2 = (|00⟩ + |11⟩)/√2 = |Φ⁺⟩

Key Insight: Why Can't We Describe Individual Qubits?

In |Φ⁺⟩ = (|00⟩+|11⟩)/√2, try to describe qubit 1 alone. Is it |0⟩? Is it |1⟩? Neither! It's in a superposition that DEPENDS on qubit 2. If we measure qubit 2 and get 0, qubit 1 "becomes" |0⟩. If we get 1, qubit 1 "becomes" |1⟩. This correlation is instantaneous regardless of distance - the essence of entanglement!
